Why I Study Extreme Weather

📅 March 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Pinkrah Nana Ofosuhene

I didn't choose atmospheric science. It chose me — or rather, Ghana's university placement system did, dropping me into meteorology when I had my sights set on computer engineering. But somewhere between the first synoptic chart and the first Python script that mapped a temperature anomaly, I stopped being someone who had been redirected and started being someone who was exactly where they needed to be.

The Night That Changed Everything

In February 2024, I was in Kumasi, Ghana, and I noticed something wrong. People couldn't sleep. Friends were texting at 2am, 3am — not because they were night owls, but because it was simply too hot to close their eyes. This wasn't a heatwave in the conventional sense (daytime highs were only moderately elevated), but the nighttime temperatures refused to drop. The minimum daily temperatures were staying abnormally high.

"I personally noticed people having sleepless nights — and then I traced it in the station data. The signal was right there."

That was the moment where science became something urgent. I wasn't just analyzing historical records anymore — I was looking at a phenomenon that was actively disrupting people's lives, sleep, and health. And if it was doing this in Kumasi, what was it doing in Mali, in Niger, in the more arid regions of the Sahel?

The Mali Wake-Up Call

A few months later, a heat event in Mali killed over 100 people. To most of the world, it was a footnote. To me, it was a reason. West Africa is warming faster than the global average in some metrics, and the region has some of the least robust early-warning systems on the planet. The gap between what atmospheric science knows and what communities on the ground receive is enormous — and that gap costs lives.

My undergraduate final-year project on heatwave analysis in Ghana scored 89/100. More importantly, it was submitted for peer review. Not because I needed a grade. Because the findings mattered.

From Ghana to the Ohio River Basin

Today I'm at Western Kentucky University, studying severe-storm environments across the Ohio River Basin using ERA5 reanalysis and CMIP6 climate projections. The geography is different; the urgency is the same. Climate change is shifting when and where extreme weather happens — tornadoes, derechos, extreme precipitation events — and the communities of Kentucky and the broader Ohio Valley need robust, data-driven science to prepare.

Extreme weather is the common thread. Whether it's a heatwave in West Africa stripping agricultural workers of their ability to work during daylight hours, or a tornado outbreak in the Ohio Valley that communities couldn't anticipate — the tools are the same: good data, careful diagnostics, and the courage to communicate findings clearly.

Why You Should Care Too

If you're reading this, you probably already care about climate. But I'd ask you to think about where you sit in the chain between science and impact. Atmospheric science produces forecasts and projections every day that could, in principle, save lives. Whether they actually do depends on whether those findings leave the pages of journals and reach the people who need them.

That's the kind of atmospheric scientist I want to be. Not just one who publishes. One who bridges.
