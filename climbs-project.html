MS Thesis | NSF EPSCoR CLIMBS

Climatology and Future Projection of Mesoscale Severe-Storm Environments in the Ohio River Basin Derived from ERA5 and A Downscaled CMIP6

Thesis proposal title and project scope from local thesis proposal materials, WKU abstract text, and defense notes.

Scientific Objective

Quantify the climatology, spatial distribution, and temporal variability of mesoscale severe-storm-supportive environments across the Ohio River Basin.

Datasets

ERA5 reanalysis, NOAA Storm Events Database, downscaled CMIP6 climate projections, and derived severe-weather diagnostics.

Methods

Event-environment matching, diagnostic computation, ERA5-CMIP6 overlap evaluation, calibration, and Random Forest classification.

Research Output

Historical climatologies, validation figures, probability maps, feature-importance interpretation, and future severe-storm environment projections.

Research Context

The Ohio River Basin experiences frequent severe convective hazards, including tornadoes, damaging winds, and large hail. My thesis addresses a documented gap by focusing on the environmental conditions that support these events, rather than using storm reports alone.

The framework uses thermodynamic, kinematic, moisture-related, and composite diagnostics including CAPE, CIN, vertical wind shear, storm-relative helicity, lapse rates, vorticity, Significant Tornado Parameter, and Supercell Composite Parameter.

Technical Workflow

  • Derive severe-weather diagnostics from ERA5 reanalysis over a multi-decadal historical period.
  • Match environmental fields to NOAA Storm Events labels for tornado, large hail, and damaging-wind occurrence.
  • Train and evaluate Random Forest classifiers to estimate severe-storm-supportive environments.
  • Evaluate downscaled CMIP6 against ERA5 over the overlap period and apply calibrated relationships to future scenarios.
